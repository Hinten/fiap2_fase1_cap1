Abstract
Background: Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is increasingly recognized as a potential risk factor for ischemic heart 
disease (IHD), but its role remains underexplored. This study investigated the association between OSA and IHD 
using a case-control design.
Objectives:  The objective of this study was to establish OSA as a risk factor for developing IHD.
Methods:  A total of 122 participants were recruited and categorized into two groups: patients with a recent history 
of IHD (< 7 days) (wIHD group, n = 61) and controls without IHD but with hypertension (cIHD group, n = 61). The 
STOP-Bang questionnaire was used to assess OSA risk, and statistical analyses, including odds ratio (OR), relative 
risk (RR), and attributable risk (AR), were performed. A significance level of 0.05 was adopted for all statistical 
analyses. 
Results: Patients in the wIHD group had significantly higher STOP-Bang scores than the control group (p < 0.01), 
with an AR of 71.929% and an OR of 40.078. Body mass index showed a significant positive correlation with OSA 
risk (p = 0.0016, r = 0.396).The OR/RR was calculated at 11.25 (> 1). The level of statistical significance was found 
to be 0.05.
Conclusion:  This study identifies OSA as an important risk factor for IHD, supporting the need for routine OSA 
screening in cardiovascular risk assessment. Larger studies are needed to further explore this relationship
Keywords: Myocardial Ischemia; Obstructive Sleep Apnea; Case-Control Studies; Risk Factors.

Ischemic heart disease (IHD) has been widely and 
repetitively studied in medical science from morphological 
and clinical perspectives. However, the correlation of IHD or 
ischemia as a pathological process with obstructive sleep apnea 
(OSA) has long been neglected from the aspects of research in 
clinical medicine until recently. Studies have already shown 
OSA to be related with chronic heart failure. The pathogenesis 
of this phenomenon has been investigated to be fat deposition 
on the upper body, including visceral fat, tongue, and throat.1 
Moreover, fluid translocation from the lower extremities to the 
neck has been associated with vascular congestion and edema 
of the pharyngeal area, and these morphological changes 
contribute to exacerbation of decompensation in heart failure.2

Method
In this study, patients hospitalized in the cardiology 
department of City Clinic Hospital No. 31, Moscow with a 
recent history of IHD not exceeding 7 days were included. 
Patients included were diagnosed with ST-segment elevation 
myocardial infarction, non-ST-segment elevation myocardial 
infarction, stable angina, and unstable angina. The clinical data 
of these patients were compared with patients without IHD in 
their anamnesis. For assessment of risk of developing OSA, the 
STOP-Bang score questionnaire was used, and patients were 
interviewed individually by the authors with signed consent. In 
this questionnaire, subjects are assessed on the following eight 
criteria: snoring, tiredness or fatigability, observed stopped 
breathing or choking during sleep, arterial blood pressure, 
body mass index (BMI) > 35 kg/m2, age older than 50 years, 
neck size measured around Adam’s apple or the laryngeal 
prominence of the thyroid cartilage, and sex (with male gaining 
one point). On the score system, 0 to 2 indicates a low risk 
of developing OSA; 3 to 4 indicates an intermediate risk of 
developing OSA; and 5 to 8 indicates a high risk of developing 
OSA.8 A retrospective study and comparison of individual 
patient histories including anamnesis and comorbidities was 
also carried out simultaneously.
Statistical analysis
A total of 122 patients (N = 122) were assessed in this 
study, as calculated statistical power was observed to be 
greater than 99.99% with this sample size, and the sample size 
taken is greater than in previously conducted studies on the topic. In order to conduct a case-control study, the patients 
were divided into two equal groups: 61 patients with recent 
history of IHD were grouped and denominated “wIHD,” and 
the control group consisted of 61 patients without any history 
of IHD but with chronic arterial hypertension, denominated 
“cIHD.” Chronic arterial hypertension was chosen as a 
baseline characteristic as it is a comorbidity factor and, in 
this case, a dependent variable. Continuous variables that 
follow a normal distribution are presented as the mean plus or 
minus the standard deviation. On the other hand, continuous 
variables without a normal distribution are presented as the 
median and interquartile range (IQR). Categorical variables 
are expressed as the count and corresponding percentage. 
Normality of continuous variables was evaluated using the 
Shapiro-Wilk test. Differences in proportions between groups 
were assessed with the two-proportion Z test. Receiver 
operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was conducted 
to determine the diagnostic accuracy of the STOP-Bang score. 
Relationships between continuous variables were examined 
using Pearson’s correlation coefficient and simple linear 
regression. Associations between categorical variables were 
tested with the chi-square test. All statistical analyses were 
carried out in STATSOFT Statistica version 13.3,9 adopting a 
significance level (alpha) of 0.05. There were no missing data 
for the variables analyzed.

discussion and discourse in medical science, the correlation

in clinical research. Very few studies have been conducted in 
this regard to date. In patients prescribed continuous positive 
airway pressure (CPAP) treatment for OSA, the non-compliant 
subjects with severe and moderate OSA were observed to

finding in patients after acute myocardial infarction. The 
SAPAMI study also found that, using apnea-hypopnea index

apnea.12 Considering ischemia as a pathological process of 
inadequate end-organ oxygen supply,13 OSA has been found 
to be a comorbid factor in ischemic stroke and transient ischemic attacks in the United States of America and a 
significant contributing factor to silent cerebral infarctions 
in Japan.14 The discussion of correlation between OSA 
and IHD is incomplete without recognizing the circadian 
rhythm and physiological changes during the sleep cycle. 
In genetically modified animal models, factors like light 
intensity, small molecules that maintain circadian rhythm,

The paradoxical phase of sleep or REM phase plays a 
considerable role in understanding the relation between 
OSA and IHD. According to electroencephalography 
studies, during the REM phase of sleep, the body undergoes 
many physiological changes. Onset of arrhythmia, increase 
in brain temperature and cerebral blood flow, muscle 
atonia,16 and a rapid rise in blood pressure were noticed.17 
Lung volumes are decreased during REM and between 
REM and non-REM phases of sleep; end-expiratory volume 
is decreased significantly, even if on CPAP therapy.18 
Moreover, patients with non-dipping blood pressure have 
been linked to development of ischemic diseases including 
IHD and transient ischemic attacks, and interleukin-2 
mediated systemic inflammation development.19,20 In case 
of ischemic changes, the volumetric oxygen consumption 
by skeletal muscles is permanently decreased,21 along with 
increased higher baseline oxygen saturation, decreased 
lower plateau in oxygen saturation, lowered oxygen 
saturation under curve, prolonged time to reach oxygen 
saturation plateau, and prolonged time to increase oxygen 
saturation due to abnormal microvascular reperfusion.22 
Hence, these considerations, added to the results of the 
present case-control study, establish an association between 
IHD and risk of developing OSA. Further research in this 
field with a longitudinal study is very likely to establish OSA 
as a risk factor of IHD. Being associated with IHD, patients 
with established OSA can be screened for probable IHD, 
or OSA might be used as a determinant of developing IHD 
with the help of artificial intelligence based on machine 
learning models. This study shows AR of 71.929%; smoking 
has a similar AR for the occurrence of myocardial infarction. 
Therefore, with further study, OSA can be added to risk 
stratification for IHD or acute coronary syndrome.23 Risk stratification might be done by using OSA, defined in stages 
from mild to moderate, which can be added to scales like 
the Systematic Coronary Risk Evaluation 2 (SCORE2),24 after 
further large-scale evaluation. 
Limitations
The study was conducted with minimal resources and 
over a short duration of 6 months. The study has indicated 
that OSA is positively related with risk of developing IHD; 
however, in order to establish it as a risk factor for IHD, 
it is necessary to conduct a longitudinal study among a 
sufficiently large population. Although the control group 
was isolated from risk factors including smoking and 
alcohol consumption, medications were not stopped. 
Polysomnography was not conducted to diagnose OSA, 
but STOP-Bang was used to determine higher probability 
of OSA.  
Conclusion
IHD is associated with development of OSA. This case-
control study has effectively established this association 
with statistically viable data. The risk of developing OSA 
after an IHD event in the wIHD group was 2 points higher 
on the STOP-Bang score and was statistically significant, 
with a probability value less than 0.01 (p < 0.05). It was 
additionally found that, on occurrence of IHD, AR was 
71.929%. Hence, it is essential in the clinical research 
of cardiology and sleep medicine to recognize OSA as a 
factor of importance in risk assessment of IHD and other 
ischemic processes. From the point of view of future 
research, a large-scale population study on the topic 
addressed in this study should be conducted.
