Abstract
Background:  Coronary artery disease (CAD) remains a leading cause of death worldwide, with inflammation 
playing a key role in its pathophysiology. Inflammatory biomarkers may offer valuable insights into disease 
mechanisms and risk stratification, potentially informing therapeutic decisions.
Objectives:  This study aimed to investigate the association between key inflammatory markers — C-reactive 
protein (CRP), neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR), and platelet-to-lymphocyte ratio (PLR) — and both the 
presence and severity of coronary artery stenosis (CAS) in patients with suspected CAD undergoing coronary 
angiography.
Methods:  This retrospective study included 767 patients referred for coronary angiography due to suspected CAD. 
The primary outcome was the presence of significant CAS. Demographic data, medical history, and blood samples 
were collected. Statistical analyses included descriptive methods and regression modeling as well as receiver 
operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis to determine optimal cutoff values for biomarkers, with area under 
the curve (AUC) used to assess predictive performance. A p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.
Results:  Of the 767 patients (mean age 58.3±12.4 years; 56.7% men), 496 had significant CAS (≥ 50%) and 271 
had nonsignificant CAS (< 50%). Levels of CRP (0.44 vs. 0.13 mg/dL, p = 0.01) and NLR (2.6 vs. 2.0, p = 0.05) 
were significantly higher in patients with CAS. No significant difference was observed for PLR (129.4 vs. 118.6,  
p = 0.13). In multivariable analysis, CRP (OR = 1.188, p = 0.044) and NLR (OR=1.567, p = 0.03) were identified 
as independent predictors of significant CAS. However, ROC curve analysis showed limited discriminative ability 
for both CRP (AUC = 0.526) and NLR (AUC = 0.510), with values only slightly above chance.
Conclusion:  Increased levels of CRP and NLR are significantly associated with CAS and may serve as potential 
biomarkers for detecting CAD.
Keywords:  Coronary Artery Disease; C-Reactive Protein; Biomarcadores .

Coronary artery disease (CAD) remains a leading cause 
of morbidity and mortality worldwide, primarily due to the 
progression of atherosclerosis and its associated inflammatory 
mechanisms.1 Identifying reliable biomarkers for the early 
detection and progression of coronary artery stenosis (CAS) — 
a key clinical manifestation of CAD — is essential to enhance 
diagnostic precision and inform therapeutic strategies.2Systemic inflammation and lipid dysregulation are well-
established contributors to CAD pathogenesis.3 Biomarkers 
such as C-reactive protein (CRP) have been widely investigated 
for their ability to reflect inflammatory activity linked to 
atherosclerosis.4 In addition, novel composite indices — 
e.g., the neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR), platelet-to-
lymphocyte ratio (PLR), systemic immune-inflammation index 
(SII), and atherogenic index of plasma (AIP) — have emerged 
as potential indicators of cardiovascular risk.5-6 These markers, 
derived from routine laboratory tests, offer a cost-effective, 
accessible means of evaluating inflammation and lipid-related 
cardiovascular risk in clinical practice.7
Despite growing interest, the predictive value of these 
inflammatory and atherogenic markers for identifying 
the presence and severity of CAS remains under active 
investigation.8 Moreover, the influence of demographic and 
clinical variables — including sex-based differences — on 
these biomarkers is not yet fully understood.9 Negative 
findings in this area are particularly valuable, as they help 
prevent premature clinical adoption of ineffective markers

This retrospective observational study seeks to address 
these knowledge gaps by evaluating the relationship between 
inflammatory and atherogenic markers and CAS in patients 
with suspected CAD undergoing coronary angiography. By 
analyzing a range of biomarkers — including CRP , NLR, PLR, 
SII, and AIP — and comparing their predictive value across

utility in detecting significant CAS. The findings aim to enhance 
the clinical understanding and application of these biomarkers

Methods
Study design
This retrospective observational study was conducted 
to evaluate the association between inflammatory and 
atherogenic biomarkers and the presence of CAS. The study 
was carried out at a tertiary care center between January 
2022 and December 2023, including patients who underwent 
coronary angiography for suspected CAD during this period.Study population
All consecutive patients who underwent coronary angiography 
for suspected CAD between January 2022 and December 2023 
were screened for eligibility. Inclusion required the availability 
of complete clinical, laboratory, and angiographic data. 
Exclusion criteria were active infection; systemic inflammatory 
or autoimmune disease; malignancy; acute coronary syndrome 
(to avoid confounding elevations in inflammatory markers 
due to myocardial injury); recent use (within 1 month) of anti-
inflammatory or immunosuppressive drugs; history of organ 
transplantation; or incomplete medical records.
A total of 767 patients met the eligibility criteria and 
were included in the final analysis. Demographic data, 
comorbidities, and lifestyle factors — including smoking status 
— were systematically collected. All data were extracted from 
the hospital’s electronic health records and verified by trained 
research personnel to ensure accuracy and reliability.

Blood samples were obtained prior to coronary angiography. 
Complete blood counts were used to determine neutrophil, 
lymphocyte, and platelet counts. Biochemical analyses 
included serum levels of CRP , total cholesterol, high-density 
lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C), low-density lipoprotein 
cholesterol (LDL-C), and triglycerides.
Derived inflammatory and lipid-related markers were 
calculated as follows: i) NLR: absolute neutrophil count 
divided by absolute lymphocyte count, reflecting systemic 
inflammation; ii) PLR: platelet count divided by lymphocyte 
count, indicating the balance between thrombosis and 
inflammation; iii) systemic immune-inflammation index 
(SII): (neutrophil count × platelet count) / lymphocyte count, 
representing a comprehensive immune-inflammatory profile; 
iv) AIP: log(triglycerides / HDL-C), serving as an indicator of 
lipid-related cardiovascular risk.
All laboratory analyses were performed by using 
standardized protocols on automated analyzers in the 
hospital’s central laboratory.
Coronary angiography
Coronary angiography was conducted using standard 
fluoroscopic techniques via femoral or radial arterial access. 
Procedures were performed by experienced interventional 
cardiologists who were blinded to the patients’ clinical 
and laboratory information. The degree of CAS was 
visually assessed, with significant CAS defined as ≥ 50% 
luminal narrowing in any major epicardial coronary artery. 
Patients were then categorized into two groups: those with 
significant CAS (≥ 50%) and those without (< 50%), enabling 
comparative and regression analyses.
Subgroup analysis
A subgroup analysis was conducted in order to explore 
potential sex-related differences in inflammatory and 
atherogenic biomarkers. Men and women were compared 
with respect to CRP , NLR, PLR, SII, and AIP values. This analysis 
aimed to determine whether sex influenced the expression 
of these biomarkers.
Ethical considerations
The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the 
institutional ethics committee, in accordance with the ethical 
standards outlined in the 2024 revision of the World Medical 
Association Declaration of Helsinki. Due to the retrospective 
design of the study, the requirement for informed consent 
was waived. Patient confidentiality was safeguarded by 
anonymizing all data prior to analysis, and access to sensitive 
information was restricted to authorized study personnel.
Quality control and reliability
All measurements were conducted using calibrated 
equipment under standardized operating conditions in order 
to ensure the reliability and consistency of data. The hospital’s 
electronic health records system was audited to minimize data extraction errors. Laboratory tests were repeated when initial 
results fell outside expected ranges or showed discrepancies. 
Readings were confirmed by 2 independent cardiologists 
through consensus in order to reduce interobserver variability 
in angiographic interpretation.
Statistical analysis
Descriptive statistics were used to summarize patient 
characteristics. Continuous variables were expressed as mean 
± standard deviation (SD) for normally distributed data or as 
median and interquartile range for non-normally distributed data. 
Categorical variables were reported as frequencies (percentages).
Group comparisons (CAS vs. no CAS) were conducted using 
chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables 
abd independent samples t-test (for normally distributed 
continuous variables) or Mann-Whitney U test (for non-
normally distributed variables).
Comparisons between men and women were also 
performed using the independent samples t-test or the Mann-
Whitney U test, depending on data distribution. Normality of 
continuous variables was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test 
and visual inspection of histograms.
Univariable and multivariable logistic regression analyses 
were conducted to identify associations between predictor 
variables and CAS. Results were reported as odds ratios (ORs) 
with 95% CIs. Variables with a p-value < 0.10 in univariable 
analysis, along with clinically relevant covariates (i.e., age, 
sex, CRP , NLR, LDL-C, hypertension, diabetes, and smoking 
status), were included in the multivariable model.
Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was 
used to determine optimal cutoff values for CRP and NLR, 
with area under the curve (AUC) used to assess predictive 
performance. The Youden index (sensitivity + specificity 
− 1) was applied to identify optimal thresholds. AUC 95% 
confidence intervals were calculated using the DeLong 
method, and p-values for AUC were obtained to evaluate 
statistical significance.
All statistical tests were 2-tailed, and p-values < 0.05 were 
considered statistically significant. Analyses were performed 
using IBM SPSS Statistics for Windows, version 25 (IBM Corp., 
Armonk, N.Y., USA).
Results
A total of 767 patients undergoing coronary angiography 
were included in the study. Baseline characteristics reflected 
a representative cardiovascular risk profile. Among the cohort, 
496 patients had significant CAS (≥ 50%), while 271 did not 
(< 50%) ( Table  1). Notably, 21.4% had a prior diagnosis of 
CAD, while 35.6% and 31.7% had hypertension and diabetes 
mellitus, respectively — comorbidities known to influence 
inflammatory marker levels. Most patients (96.2%) were 
in sinus rhythm and had preserved left ventricular ejection 
fraction (mean 59.4%), indicating overall s cardiac function.
Comparative analysis between the CAS and nonCAS groups 
revealed significant differences ( Table  1). Patients with ≥ 
50% CAS had a markedly higher prevalence of established 
CAD (71.3% vs. 27.8%), reinforcing the progressive nature of

Discussion
This study investigated the predictive value of inflammatory 
and atherogenic biomarkers for identifying CAS in patients 
undergoing coronary angiography for suspected CAD. It also 
examined sex-related differences in biomarker expression to 
assess their potential relevance in risk stratification. The findings 
indicate that CRP and NLR were modestly associated with the 
presence of CAS, whereas other markers — including PLR, SII, 
and AIP — did not exhibit significant predictive value (Central 
Illustration).
Our results are consistent with some prior studies, while 
diverging from others, particularly regarding the diagnostic 
performance of inflammatory biomarkers. Some investigations 
have reported modest associations between CRP or NLR 
and angiographically confirmed CAS, with minimal value 
for PLR and related indices.11 In contrast, other studies have 
found stronger associations, which may reflect differences in 
study design, populations, and endpoint definitions.12 This 
heterogeneity highlights the need for cautious interpretation 
of biomarker data in clinical decision-making.
Among the biomarkers assessed, CRP — a well-established 
indicator of systemic inflammation — was significantly 
increased in patients with CAS13 and remained independently 
associated with CAS in multivariable analysis. Increased 
levels of CRP reflect heightened vascular inflammation 
and have been consistently linked to the pathogenesis of 
atherosclerosis.4 However, Mendelian randomization studies, 
such as those by Ridker et al.,4 suggest that CRP may act 
more as a bystander than a causal factor in atherosclerosis. 
In line with this, the diagnostic performance of CRP in our 
cohort was poor, with an AUC of 0.526 — only marginally 
above random chance.14,15 Despite statistical significance, 
these findings demonstrate that CRP has limited clinical 
utility as a standalone diagnostic tool for CAS, challenging the assumption that statistical associations necessarily confer 
predictive value.16 
NLR, a composite marker derived from routine blood counts, 
showed a modest but statistically significant association with 
CAS.4 Increased NLR reflects systemic inflammation and immune 
dysregulation, indicating a shift toward a pro-inflammatory state 
and diminished adaptive immunity.17 These findings contribute 
to the growing body of evidence supporting NLR as a potential 
marker of cardiovascular risk.5 However, the low AUC values 
observed underscore its limited standalone predictive value. 
Further studies are warranted to evaluate whether combining NLR 
with other biomarkers can enhance its diagnostic performance.18
A key observation of this study was the lack of significant 
predictive value for other inflammatory and atherogenic 
markers, including PLR, SII, and AIP . These negative findings are 
clinically relevant, challenging the assumption that all accessible 
inflammatory indices are useful for CAD risk stratification. While 
PLR, for example, has been proposed as a marker of thrombotic 
risk,19 our data do not support its utility in predicting CAS. Such 
discrepancies may arise from variations in patient selection, disease 
severity, or study methodology, which highlights the importance 
of rigorous validation before clinical implementation.
Interestingly, PLR, SII, and AIP — though previously suggested 
as markers of cardiovascular risk — did not show significant 
associations with CAS in this cohort.13,20 This may be due to 
variability in these indices across populations or methodological 
differences in study design.21 Moreover, the derived nature of 
these markers, which depend on multiple interacting laboratory 
parameters, may introduce noise that weakens their association 
with specific clinical outcomes.22

Despite the contributions of this study to understanding 
inflammatory and atherogenic markers in CAD, several limitations 
should be acknowledged. First, its retrospective design introduces 
potential selection bias and limits causal inference.26 Second, the 
analysis did not account for confounders such as medication use, 
diet, or lifestyle factors that may influence biomarker levels.27 
Third, CAS was assessed by visual estimation during angiography 
— a method subject to interobserver variability despite its common use.28 Lastly, the modest AUC values for CRP and NLR 
reinforce the need for caution in interpreting these markers in 
isolation. Combining them with advanced imaging or genetic 
data may improve diagnostic accuracy.29
Contrary to previous studies identifying age and male sex as 
consistent predictors of CAS, our analysis did not find statistically 
significant associations for these variables. This may be due to 
the relatively small sample size, which could have limited the 
statistical power to detect such effects. Additionally, the study 
population’s characteristics — including a narrow age range 
and potential selection bias toward patients with inflammatory 
profiles — may have influenced the predictive performance of 
traditional risk factors.

and limited sample size (767 patients) further constrain 
generalizability. These factors may have reduced the ability to

Importantly, the study’s negative findings have meaningful 
implications for both clinical practice and research. Negative 
studies serve a critical role in refining our understanding of disease by identifying biomarkers that fail to deliver sufficient 
predictive performance, despite theoretical promise. Our 
results clearly show that although inflammatory and atherogenic 
markers such as CRP and NLR are statistically associated with 
CAS, their diagnostic performance is poor, with AUC values near 
random chance. This highlights the need for rigorous clinical 
validation before integrating biomarkers into routine decision-
making. By documenting the limited utility of these widely 
studied indices, this study helps prevent premature adoption 
of ineffective diagnostic tools and encourages more efficient 
use of clinical and research resources.

Conclusion
This study identified significant associations between CAS 
and both prior CAD and increased levels of CRP . However, the 
poor predictive performance of CRP and other inflammatory 
markers — NLR, PLR, SII, and AIP — with diagnostic accuracy 
only marginally above chance, underscores their limited clinical 
utility for assessing CAS. These findings suggest that future research 
should move beyond these commonly studied markers and focus 
instead on novel biomarkers or integrated, multimodal strategies 
that combine clinical, biochemical, and imaging data. By clearly 
demonstrating the limited value of these inflammatory indices, 
this study contributes to refining the field, helping to redirect 
efforts and resources toward more promising avenues for effective 
risk stratification in CAD.
